Chapter IX

THE MOCK-TURTLE'S STORY

1.

'You can't think how glad I am to see you again, you dear old thing!' said the Duchess, as she tucked her arm affectionately into Alice's, and they walked off together.<<Back

2.

Alice was very glad to find her in such a pleasant temper, and thought to herself that perhaps it was only the pepper that had made her so savage when they met in the kitchen. 'When I'm a Duchess,' she said to herself, (not in a very hopeful tone though), 'I won't have any pepper in my kitchen at all. Soup does very well without--Maybe it's always pepper that makes people hot-tempered,' she went on, very much pleased at having found out a new kind of rule, 'and vinegar that makes them sour--and camomile that makes them bitter--and--and barley-sugar and such things that make children sweet-tempered. I only wish people knew that: then they wouldn't be so stingy about it, you know--'<<Back

3.

She had quite forgotten the Duchess by this time, and was a little startled when she heard her voice close to her ear. 'You're thinking about something, my dear, and that makes you forget to talk. I can't tell you just now what the moral of that is, but I shall remember it in a bit.'<<Back

4.

'Perhaps it hasn't one,' Alice ventured to remark.<<Back

5.

'Tut, tut, child!' said the Duchess. 'Everything's got a moral, if only you can find it.' And she squeezed herself up closer to Alice's side as she spoke.<<Back

6.

Alice did not much like keeping so close to her: first, because the Duchess was very ugly; and secondly, because she was exactly the right height to rest her chin upon Alice's shoulder, and it was an uncomfortably sharp chin. However, she did not like to be rude, so she bore it as well as she could.<<Back

7.

'The game's going on rather better now,' she said, by way of keeping up the conversation a little.<<Back

8.

''Tis so,' said the Duchess: 'and the moral of that is--"Oh, 'tis love, 'tis love, that makes the world go round!"'<<Back

9.

'Somebody said,' Alice whispered, 'that it's done by everybody minding their own business!'<<Back

10.

'Ah, well! It means much the same thing,' said the Duchess, digging her sharp little chin into Alice's shoulder as she added, 'and the moral of that is--"Take care of the sense, and the sounds will take care of themselves."'<<Back

11.

'How fond she is of finding morals in things!' Alice thought to herself.<<Back

12.

'I dare say you're wondering why I don't put my arm round your waist,' the Duchess said after a pause: 'the reason is, that I'm doubtful about the temper of your flamingo. Shall I try the experiment?'<<Back

13.

'He might bite,' Alice cautiously replied, not feeling at all anxious to have the experiment tried.<<Back

14.

'Very true,' said the Duchess: 'flamingoes and mustard both bite. And the moral of that is--"Birds of a feather flock together."'<<Back

15.

'Only mustard isn't a bird,' Alice remarked.<<Back

16.

'Right, as usual,' said the Duchess: 'what a clear way you have of putting things!'<<Back

17.

'It's a mineral, I think,' said Alice.<<Back

18.

'Of course it is,' said the Duchess, who seemed ready to agree to everything that Alice said; 'there's a large mustard-mine near here. And the moral of that is--"The more there is of mine, the less there is of yours."'<<Back

19.

'Oh, I know!' exclaimed Alice, who had not attended to this last remark, 'it's a vegetable. It doesn't look like one, but it is.'<<Back

20.

'I quite agree with you,' said the Duchess; 'and the moral of that is--"Be what you would seem to be"--or if you'd like it put more simply--"Never imagine yourself not to be otherwise than what it might appear to others that what you were or might have been was not otherwise than what you had been would have appeared to them to be otherwise."'<<Back

21.

'I think I should understand that better,' Alice said very politely, 'if I had it written down: but I can't quite follow it as you say it.'<<Back

22.

'That's nothing to what I could say if I chose,' the Duchess replied, in a pleased tone.<<Back

23.

'Pray don't trouble yourself to say it any longer than that,' said Alice.<<Back

24.

'Oh, don't talk about trouble!' said the Duchess. 'I make you a present of everything I've said as yet.'<<Back

25.

'A cheap sort of present!' thought Alice. 'I'm glad they don't give birthday presents like that!' But she did not venture to say it out loud.<<Back

26.

'Thinking again?' the Duchess asked, with another dig of her sharp little chin.<<Back

27.

'I've a right to think,' said Alice sharply, for she was beginning to feel a little worried.<<Back

28.

'Just about as much right,' said the Duchess, 'as pigs have to fly; and the m--'<<Back

29.

But here, to Alice's great surprise, the Duchess's voice died away, even in the middle of her favourite word 'moral,' and the arm that was linked into hers began to tremble. Alice looked up, and there stood the Queen in front of them, with her arms folded, frowning like a thunderstorm.<<Back

30.

'A fine day, your Majesty!' the Duchess began in a low, weak voice.<<Back

31.

'Now, I give you fair warning,' shouted the Queen, stamping on the ground as she spoke; 'either you or your head must be off, and that in about half no time! Take your choice!'<<Back

32.

The Duchess took her choice, and was gone in a moment.<<Back

33.

'Let's go on with the game,' the Queen said to Alice; and Alice was too much frightened to say a word, but slowly followed her back to the croquet-ground.<<Back

34.

The other guests had taken advantage of the Queen's absence, and were resting in the shade: however, the moment they saw her, they hurried back to the game, the Queen merely remarking that a moment's delay would cost them their lives.<<Back

35.

All the time they were playing the Queen never left off quarrelling with the other players, and shouting 'Off with his head!' or 'Off with her head!' Those whom she sentenced were taken into custody by the soldiers, who of course had to leave off being arches to do this, so that by the end of half an hour or so there were no arches left, and all the players, except the King, the Queen, and Alice, were in custody and under sentence of execution.<<Back

36.

Then the Queen left off, quite out of breath, and said to Alice, 'Have you seen the Mock Turtle yet?'<<Back

37.

'No,' said Alice. 'I don't even know what a Mock Turtle is.'<<Back

38.

'It's the thing Mock Turtle Soup is made from,' said the Queen.<<Back

39.

'I never saw one, or heard of one,' said Alice. 'Come on, then,' said the Queen, 'and he shall tell you his history,'<<Back

40.

As they walked off together, Alice heard the King say in a low voice, to the company generally, 'You are all pardoned.' 'Come, that's a good thing!' she said to herself, for she had felt quite unhappy at the number of executions the Queen had ordered.<<Back

41.

They very soon came upon a Gryphon, lying fast asleep in the sun. (If you don't know what a Gryphon is, look at the picture.) 'Up, lazy thing!' said the Queen, 'and take this young lady to see the Mock Turtle, and to hear his history. I must go back and see after some executions I have ordered'; and she walked off, leaving Alice alone with the Gryphon. Alice did not quite like the look of the creature, but on the whole she thought it would be quite as safe to stay with it as to go after that savage Queen: so she waited.<<Back

42.

The Gryphon sat up and rubbed its eyes: then it watched the Queen till she was out of sight: then it chuckled. 'What fun!' said the Gryphon, half to itself, half to Alice.<<Back

43.

'What is the fun?' said Alice.<<Back

44.

'Why, she,' said the Gryphon. 'It's all her fancy, that: they never executes nobody, you know. Come on!'<<Back

45.

'Everybody says "come on!" here,' thought Alice, as she went slowly after it: 'I never was so ordered about in all my life, never!'<<Back

46.

They had not gone far before they saw the Mock Turtle in the distance, sitting sad and lonely on a little ledge of rock, and, as they came nearer, Alice could hear him sighing as if his heart would break. She pitied him deeply. 'What is his sorrow?' she asked the Gryphon, and the Gryphon answered, very nearly in the same words as before, 'It's all his fancy, that: he hasn't got no sorrow, you know. Come on!'<<Back

47.

So they went up to the Mock Turtle, who looked at them with large eyes full of tears, but said nothing.<<Back

48.

'This here young lady,' said the Gryphon, 'she wants for to know your history, she do.'<<Back

49.

'I'll tell it her,' said the Mock Turtle in a deep, hollow tone: 'sit down, both of you, and don't speak a word till I've finished.'<<Back

50.

So they sat down, and nobody spoke for some minutes. Alice thought to herself, 'I don't see how he can even finish, if he doesn't begin.' But she waited patiently.<<Back

51.

'Once,' said the Mock Turtle at last, with a deep sigh, 'I was a real Turtle.'<<Back

52.

These words were followed by a very long silence, broken only by an occasional exclamation of 'Hjckrrh!' from the Gryphon, and the constant heavy sobbing of the Mock Turtle. Alice was very nearly getting up and saying, 'Thank you, sir, for your interesting story,' but she could not help thinking there must be more to come, so she sat still and said nothing.<<Back

53.

'When we were little,' the Mock Turtle went on at last, more calmly, though still sobbing a little now and then, 'we went to school in the sea. The master was an old Turtle--we used to call him Tortoise--'<<Back

54.

'Why did you call him Tortoise, if he wasn't one?' Alice asked. 'We called him Tortoise because he taught us,' said the Mock Turtle angrily: 'really you are very dull!'<<Back

55.

'You ought to be ashamed of yourself for asking such a simple question,' added the Gryphon; and then they both sat silent and looked at poor Alice, who felt ready to sink into the earth. At last the Gryphon said to the Mock Turtle, 'Drive on, old fellow! Don't be all day about it!' and he went on in these words:<<Back

56.

'Yes, we went to school in the sea, though you mayn't believe it--'<<Back

57.

'I never said I didn't!' interrupted Alice.<<Back

58.

'You did,' said the Mock Turtle.<<Back

59.

'Hold your tongue!' added the Gryphon, before Alice could speak again. The Mock Turtle went on.<<Back

60.

'We had the best of educations--in fact, we went to school every day--'<<Back

61.

'I've been to a day-school, too,' said Alice; 'you needn't be so proud as all that.'<<Back

62.

'With extras?' asked the Mock Turtle a little anxiously.<<Back

63.

'Yes,' said Alice, 'we learned French and music.'<<Back

64.

'And washing?' said the Mock Turtle.<<Back

65.

'Certainly not!' said Alice indignantly.<<Back

66.

'Ah! then yours wasn't a really good school,' said the Mock Turtle in a tone of great relief. 'Now at ours they had at the end of the bill, "French, music, and washing--extra."'<<Back

67.

'You couldn't have wanted it much,' said Alice; 'living at the bottom of the sea.'<<Back

68.

'I couldn't afford to learn it.' said the Mock Turtle with a sigh. 'I only took the regular course.'<<Back

69.

'What was that?' inquired Alice.<<Back

70.

'Reeling and Writhing, of course, to begin with,' the Mock Turtle replied; 'and then the different branches of Arithmetic-- Ambition, Distraction, Uglification, and Derision.'<<Back

71.

'I never heard of "Uglification,"' Alice ventured to say. 'What is it?'<<Back

72.

The Gryphon lifted up both its paws in surprise. 'What! Never heard of uglifying!' it exclaimed. 'You know what to beautify is, I suppose?'<<Back

73.

'Yes,' said Alice doubtfully: 'it means--to--make--anything--prettier.'<<Back

74.

'Well, then,' the Gryphon went on, 'if you don't know what to uglify is, you are a simpleton.'<<Back

75.

Alice did not feel encouraged to ask any more questions about it, so she turned to the Mock Turtle, and said 'What else had you to learn?'<<Back

76.

'Well, there was Mystery,' the Mock Turtle replied, counting off the subjects on his flappers, '--Mystery, ancient and modern, with Seaography: then Drawling--the Drawling-master was an old conger-eel, that used to come once a week: He taught us Drawling, Stretching, and Fainting in Coils.'<<Back

77.

'What was that like?' said Alice.<<Back

78.

'Well, I can't show it you myself,' the Mock Turtle said: 'I'm too stiff. And the Gryphon never learnt it.'<<Back

79.

'Hadn't time,' said the Gryphon: 'I went to the Classics master, though. He was an old crab, he was.' 'I never went to him,' the Mock Turtle said with a sigh: 'he taught Laughing and Grief, they used to say.'<<Back

80.

'So he did, so he did,' said the Gryphon, sighing in his turn; and both creatures hid their faces in their paws.<<Back

81.

'And how many hours a day did you do lessons?' said Alice, in a hurry to change the subject.<<Back

82.

'Ten hours the first day,' said the Mock Turtle: 'nine the next, and so on.'<<Back

83.

'What a curious plan!' exclaimed Alice.<<Back

84.

'That's the reason they're called lessons,' the Gryphon remarked: 'because they lessen from day to day.'<<Back

85.

This was quite a new idea to Alice, and she thought it over a little before she made her next remark. 'Then the eleventh day must have been a holiday?'<<Back

86.

'Of course it was,' said the Mock Turtle.<<Back

87.

'And how did you manage on the twelfth?' Alice went on eagerly.<<Back

88.

'That's enough about lessons,' the Gryphon interrupted in a very decided tone: 'tell her something about the games now.'<<Back
