Chapter XI

WHO STOLE THE TARTS?

1.

The King and Queen of Hearts were seated on their throne when they arrived, with a great crowd assembled about them--all sorts of little birds and beasts, as well as the whole pack of cards: the Knave was standing before them, in chains, with a soldier on each side to guard him; and near the King was the White Rabbit, with a trumpet in one hand, and a scroll of parchment in the other. In the very middle of the court was a table, with a large dish of tarts upon it: they looked so good, that it made Alice quite hungry to look at them--'I wish they'd get the trial done,' she thought, 'and hand round the refreshments!' But there seemed to be no chance of this, so she began looking at everything about her, to pass away the time.<<Back

2.

Alice had never been in a court of justice before, but she had read about them in books, and she was quite pleased to find that she knew the name of nearly everything there. 'That's the judge,' she said to herself, 'because of his great wig.'<<Back

3.

The judge, by the way, was the King; and as he wore his crown over the wig, (look at the frontispiece if you want to see how he did it,) he did not look at all comfortable, and it was certainly not becoming.<<Back

4.

'And that's the jury-box,' thought Alice, 'and those twelve creatures,' (she was obliged to say 'creatures,' you see, because some of them were animals, and some were birds,) 'I suppose they are the jurors.' She said this last word two or three times over to herself, being rather proud of it: for she thought, and rightly too, that very few little girls of her age knew the meaning of it at all. However, 'jury-men' would have done just as well.<<Back

5.

The twelve jurors were all writing very busily on slates. 'What are they doing?' Alice whispered to the Gryphon. 'They can't have anything to put down yet, before the trial's begun.'<<Back

6.

'They're putting down their names,' the Gryphon whispered in reply, 'for fear they should forget them before the end of the trial.'<<Back

7.

'Stupid things!' Alice began in a loud, indignant voice, but she stopped hastily, for the White Rabbit cried out, 'Silence in the court!' and the King put on his spectacles and looked anxiously round, to make out who was talking.<<Back

8.

Alice could see, as well as if she were looking over their shoulders, that all the jurors were writing down 'stupid things!' on their slates, and she could even make out that one of them didn't know how to spell 'stupid,' and that he had to ask his neighbour to tell him. 'A nice muddle their slates'll be in before the trial's over!' thought Alice.<<Back

9.

One of the jurors had a pencil that squeaked. This of course, Alice could not stand, and she went round the court and got behind him, and very soon found an opportunity of taking it away. She did it so quickly that the poor little juror (it was Bill, the Lizard) could not make out at all what had become of it; so, after hunting all about for it, he was obliged to write with one finger for the rest of the day; and this was of very little use, as it left no mark on the slate.<<Back

10.

'Herald, read the accusation!' said the King.<<Back

11.

On this the White Rabbit blew three blasts on the trumpet, and then unrolled the parchment scroll, and read as follows:--<<Back

12.

'The Queen of Hearts, she made some tarts, All on a summer day: The Knave of Hearts, he stole those tarts, And took them quite away!'<<Back

13.

'Consider your verdict,' the King said to the jury.<<Back

14.

'Not yet, not yet!' the Rabbit hastily interrupted. 'There's a great deal to come before that!'<<Back

15.

'Call the first witness,' said the King; and the White Rabbit blew three blasts on the trumpet, and called out, 'First witness!'<<Back

16.

The first witness was the Hatter. He came in with a teacup in one hand and a piece of bread-and-butter in the other. 'I beg pardon, your Majesty,' he began, 'for bringing these in: but I hadn't quite finished my tea when I was sent for.'<<Back

17.

'You ought to have finished,' said the King. 'When did you begin?'<<Back

18.

The Hatter looked at the March Hare, who had followed him into the court, arm-in-arm with the Dormouse. 'Fourteenth of March, I think it was,' he said.<<Back

19.

'Fifteenth,' said the March Hare.<<Back

20.

'Sixteenth,' added the Dormouse.<<Back

21.

'Write that down,' the King said to the jury, and the jury eagerly wrote down all three dates on their slates, and then added them up, and reduced the answer to shillings and pence.<<Back

22.

'Take off your hat,' the King said to the Hatter.<<Back

23.

'It isn't mine,' said the Hatter.<<Back

24.

'Stolen!' the King exclaimed, turning to the jury, who instantly made a memorandum of the fact.<<Back

25.

'I keep them to sell,' the Hatter added as an explanation; 'I've none of my own. I'm a hatter.'<<Back

26.

Here the Queen put on her spectacles, and began staring at the Hatter, who turned pale and fidgeted.<<Back

27.

'Give your evidence,' said the King; 'and don't be nervous, or I'll have you executed on the spot.'<<Back

28.

This did not seem to encourage the witness at all: he kept shifting from one foot to the other, looking uneasily at the Queen, and in his confusion he bit a large piece out of his teacup instead of the bread-and-butter.<<Back

29.

Just at this moment Alice felt a very curious sensation, which puzzled her a good deal until she made out what it was: she was beginning to grow larger again, and she thought at first she would get up and leave the court; but on second thoughts she decided to remain where she was as long as there was room for her.<<Back

30.

'I wish you wouldn't squeeze so.' said the Dormouse, who was sitting next to her. 'I can hardly breathe.'<<Back

31.

'I can't help it,' said Alice very meekly: 'I'm growing.'<<Back

32.

'You've no right to grow here,' said the Dormouse.<<Back

33.

'Don't talk nonsense,' said Alice more boldly: 'you know you're growing too.'<<Back

34.

'Yes, but I grow at a reasonable pace,' said the Dormouse: 'not in that ridiculous fashion.' And he got up very sulkily and crossed over to the other side of the court.<<Back

35.

All this time the Queen had never left off staring at the Hatter, and, just as the Dormouse crossed the court, she said to one of the officers of the court, 'Bring me the list of the singers in the last concert!' on which the wretched Hatter trembled so, that he shook both his shoes off.<<Back

36.

'Give your evidence,' the King repeated angrily, 'or I'll have you executed, whether you're nervous or not.'<<Back

37.

'I'm a poor man, your Majesty,' the Hatter began, in a trembling voice, '--and I hadn't begun my tea--not above a week or so--and what with the bread-and-butter getting so thin--and the twinkling of the tea--'<<Back

38.

'The twinkling of the what?' said the King.<<Back

39.

'It began with the tea,' the Hatter replied.<<Back

40.

'Of course twinkling begins with a T!' said the King sharply. 'Do you take me for a dunce? Go on!'<<Back

41.

'I'm a poor man,' the Hatter went on, 'and most things twinkled after that--only the March Hare said--'<<Back

42.

'I didn't!' the March Hare interrupted in a great hurry.<<Back

43.

'You did!' said the Hatter.<<Back

44.

'I deny it!' said the March Hare.<<Back

45.

'He denies it,' said the King: 'leave out that part.'<<Back

46.

'Well, at any rate, the Dormouse said--' the Hatter went on, looking anxiously round to see if he would deny it too: but the Dormouse denied nothing, being fast asleep.<<Back

47.

'After that,' continued the Hatter, 'I cut some more bread- and-butter--'<<Back

48.

'But what did the Dormouse say?' one of the jury asked.<<Back

49.

'That I can't remember,' said the Hatter.<<Back

50.

'You must remember,' remarked the King, 'or I'll have you executed.'<<Back

51.

The miserable Hatter dropped his teacup and bread-and-butter, and went down on one knee. 'I'm a poor man, your Majesty,' he began.<<Back

52.

'You're a very poor speaker,' said the King.<<Back

53.

Here one of the guinea-pigs cheered, and was immediately suppressed by the officers of the court. (As that is rather a hard word, I will just explain to you how it was done. They had a large canvas bag, which tied up at the mouth with strings: into this they slipped the guinea-pig, head first, and then sat upon it.)<<Back

54.

'I'm glad I've seen that done,' thought Alice. 'I've so often read in the newspapers, at the end of trials, "There was some attempts at applause, which was immediately suppressed by the officers of the court," and I never understood what it meant till now.'<<Back

55.

'If that's all you know about it, you may stand down,' continued the King.<<Back

56.

'I can't go no lower,' said the Hatter: 'I'm on the floor, as it is.'<<Back

57.

'Then you may sit down,' the King replied.<<Back

58.

Here the other guinea-pig cheered, and was suppressed.<<Back

59.

'Come, that finished the guinea-pigs!' thought Alice. 'Now we shall get on better.'<<Back

60.

'I'd rather finish my tea,' said the Hatter, with an anxious look at the Queen, who was reading the list of singers.<<Back

61.

'You may go,' said the King, and the Hatter hurriedly left the court, without even waiting to put his shoes on.<<Back

62.

'--and just take his head off outside,' the Queen added to one of the officers: but the Hatter was out of sight before the officer could get to the door.<<Back

63.

'Call the next witness!' said the King.<<Back

64.

The next witness was the Duchess's cook. She carried the pepper-box in her hand, and Alice guessed who it was, even before she got into the court, by the way the people near the door began sneezing all at once.<<Back

65.

'Give your evidence,' said the King.<<Back

66.

'Shan't,' said the cook.<<Back

67.

The King looked anxiously at the White Rabbit, who said in a low voice, 'Your Majesty must cross-examine this witness.'<<Back

68.

'Well, if I must, I must,' the King said, with a melancholy air, and, after folding his arms and frowning at the cook till his eyes were nearly out of sight, he said in a deep voice,<<Back

69.

'What are tarts made of?'<<Back

70.

'Pepper, mostly,' said the cook.<<Back

71.

'Treacle,' said a sleepy voice behind her.<<Back

72.

'Collar that Dormouse,' the Queen shrieked out. 'Behead that Dormouse! Turn that Dormouse out of court! Suppress him! Pinch him! Off with his whiskers!'<<Back

73.

For some minutes the whole court was in confusion, getting the Dormouse turned out, and, by the time they had settled down again, the cook had disappeared.<<Back

74.

'Never mind!' said the King, with an air of great relief. 'Call the next witness.' And he added in an undertone to the Queen, 'Really, my dear, you must cross-examine the next witness. It quite makes my forehead ache!'<<Back

75.

Alice watched the White Rabbit as he fumbled over the list, feeling very curious to see what the next witness would be like, '--for they haven't got much evidence yet,' she said to herself. Imagine her surprise, when the White Rabbit read out, at the top of his shrill little voice, the name 'Alice!'<<Back
