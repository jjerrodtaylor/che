Chapter V

ADVICE FROM A CATERPILLAR

1.

The Caterpillar and Alice looked at each other for some time in silence: at last the Caterpillar took the hookah out of its mouth, and addressed her in a languid, sleepy voice.<<Back

2.

'Who are you?' said the Caterpillar.<<Back

3.

This was not an encouraging opening for a conversation. Alice replied, rather shyly, 'I--I hardly know, sir, just at present-- at least I know who I was when I got up this morning, but I think I must have been changed several times since then.'<<Back

4.

'What do you mean by that?' said the Caterpillar sternly. 'Explain yourself!'<<Back

5.

'I can't explain myself, I'm afraid, sir' said Alice, 'because I'm not myself, you see.'<<Back

6.

'I don't see,' said the Caterpillar.<<Back

7.

'I'm afraid I can't put it more clearly,' Alice replied very politely, 'for I can't understand it myself to begin with; and being so many different sizes in a day is very confusing.'<<Back

8.

'It isn't,' said the Caterpillar.<<Back

9.

'Well, perhaps you haven't found it so yet,' said Alice; 'but when you have to turn into a chrysalis--you will some day, you know--and then after that into a butterfly, I should think you'll feel it a little queer, won't you?'<<Back

10.

'Not a bit,' said the Caterpillar.<<Back

11.

'Well, perhaps your feelings may be different,' said Alice; 'all I know is, it would feel very queer to me.'<<Back

12.

'You!' said the Caterpillar contemptuously. 'Who are you?'<<Back

13.

Which brought them back again to the beginning of the conversation. Alice felt a little irritated at the Caterpillar's making such very short remarks, and she drew herself up and said, very gravely, 'I think, you ought to tell me who you are, first.'<<Back

14.

'Why?' said the Caterpillar.<<Back

15.

Here was another puzzling question; and as Alice could not think of any good reason, and as the Caterpillar seemed to be in a very unpleasant state of mind, she turned away.<<Back

16.

'Come back!' the Caterpillar called after her. 'I've something important to say!'<<Back

17.

This sounded promising, certainly: Alice turned and came back again.<<Back

18.

'Keep your temper,' said the Caterpillar.<<Back

19.

'Is that all?' said Alice, swallowing down her anger as well as she could.<<Back

20.

'No,' said the Caterpillar.<<Back

21.

Alice thought she might as well wait, as she had nothing else to do, and perhaps after all it might tell her something worth hearing. For some minutes it puffed away without speaking, but at last it unfolded its arms, took the hookah out of its mouth again, and said, 'So you think you're changed, do you?'<<Back

22.

'I'm afraid I am, sir,' said Alice; 'I can't remember things as I used--and I don't keep the same size for ten minutes together!'<<Back

23.

'Can't remember what things?' said the Caterpillar.<<Back

24.

'Well, I've tried to say "How doth the little busy bee," but it all came different!' Alice replied in a very melancholy voice.<<Back

25.

'Repeat, "you are old, Father William,"' said the Caterpillar.<<Back

26.

Alice folded her hands, and began:--<<Back

27.

'You are old, Father William,' the young man said,<<Back

28.

'And your hair has become very white;<<Back

29.

And yet you incessantly stand on your head — Do you think, at your age, it is right?'<<Back

30.

'In my youth,' Father William replied to his son, <<Back

31.

'I feared it might injure the brain;<<Back

32.

But, now that I'm perfectly sure I have none,<<Back

33.

Why, I do it again and again.'<<Back

34.

'You are old,' said the youth, 'as I mentioned before,<<Back

35.

And have grown most uncommonly fat;<<Back

36.

Yet you turned a back-somersault in at the door— <<Back

37.

Pray, what is the reason of that?'<<Back

38.

'In my youth,' said the sage, as he shook his grey locks,<<Back

39.

'I kept all my limbs very supple<<Back

40.

By the use of this ointment--one shilling the box-<<Back

41.

Allow me to sell you a couple?'<<Back

42.

'You are old,' said the youth, 'and your jaws are too weak<<Back

43.

For anything tougher than suet;<<Back

44.

Yet you finished the goose, with the bones and the beak— <<Back

45.

Pray how did you manage to do it?'<<Back

46.

'In my youth,' said his father, 'I took to the law,<<Back

47.

And argued each case with my wife;<<Back

48.

And the muscular strength, which it gave to my jaw,<<Back

49.

Has lasted the rest of my life.'<<Back

50.

'You are old,' said the youth, 'one would hardly suppose<<Back

51.

That your eye was as steady as ever;<<Back

52.

Yet you balanced an eel on the end of your nose—<<Back

53.

What made you so awfully clever?'<<Back

54.

'I have answered three questions, and that is enough,' Said his father; 'don't give yourself airs! Do you think I can listen all day to such stuff?<<Back

55.

Be off, or I'll kick you down stairs!'<<Back

56.

'That is not said right,' said the Caterpillar.<<Back

57.

'Not quite right, I'm afraid,' said Alice, timidly; 'some of the words have got altered.'<<Back

58.

'It is wrong from beginning to end,' said the Caterpillar decidedly, and there was silence for some minutes.<<Back

59.

The Caterpillar was the first to speak.<<Back

60.

'What size do you want to be?' it asked.<<Back

61.

'Oh, I'm not particular as to size,' Alice hastily replied; 'only one doesn't like changing so often, you know.'<<Back

62.

'I don't know,' said the Caterpillar.<<Back

63.

Alice said nothing: she had never been so much contradicted in her life before, and she felt that she was losing her temper.<<Back

64.

'Are you content now?' said the Caterpillar.<<Back

65.

'Well, I should like to be a little larger, sir, if you wouldn't mind,' said Alice: 'three inches is such a wretched height to be.'<<Back

66.

'It is a very good height indeed!' said the Caterpillar angrily, rearing itself upright as it spoke (it was exactly three inches high).<<Back

67.

'But I'm not used to it!' pleaded poor Alice in a piteous tone. And she thought of herself, 'I wish the creatures wouldn't be so easily offended!'<<Back

68.

'You'll get used to it in time,' said the Caterpillar; and it put the hookah into its mouth and began smoking again.<<Back

69.

This time Alice waited patiently until it chose to speak again. In a minute or two the Caterpillar took the hookah out of its mouth and yawned once or twice, and shook itself. Then it got down off the mushroom, and crawled away in the grass, merely remarking as it went, 'One side will make you grow taller, and the other side will make you grow shorter.'<<Back

70.

'One side of what? The other side of what?' thought Alice to herself.<<Back

71.

'Of the mushroom,' said the Caterpillar, just as if she had asked it aloud; and in another moment it was out of sight.<<Back

72.

Alice remained looking thoughtfully at the mushroom for a minute, trying to make out which were the two sides of it; and as it was perfectly round, she found this a very difficult question. However, at last she stretched her arms round it as far as they would go, and broke off a bit of the edge with each hand.<<Back

73.

'And now which is which?' she said to herself, and nibbled a little of the right-hand bit to try the effect: the next moment she felt a violent blow underneath her chin: it had struck her foot!<<Back

74.

She was a good deal frightened by this very sudden change, but she felt that there was no time to be lost, as she was shrinking rapidly; so she set to work at once to eat some of the other bit. Her chin was pressed so closely against her foot, that there was hardly room to open her mouth; but she did it at last, and managed to swallow a morsel of the lefthand bit.<<Back

75.

'Come, my head's free at last!' said Alice in a tone of delight, which changed into alarm in another moment, when she found that her shoulders were nowhere to be found: all she could see, when she looked down, was an immense length of neck, which seemed to rise like a stalk out of a sea of green leaves that lay far below her.<<Back

76.

'What can all that green stuff be?' said Alice. 'And where have my shoulders got to? And oh, my poor hands, how is it I can't see you?' She was moving them about as she spoke, but no result seemed to follow, except a little shaking among the distant green leaves.<<Back

77.

As there seemed to be no chance of getting her hands up to her head, she tried to get her head down to them, and was delighted to find that her neck would bend about easily in any direction, like a serpent. She had just succeeded in curving it down into a graceful zigzag, and was going to dive in among the leaves, which she found to be nothing but the tops of the trees under which she had been wandering, when a sharp hiss made her draw back in a hurry: a large pigeon had flown into her face, and was beating her violently with its wings.<<Back

78.

'Serpent!' screamed the Pigeon.<<Back

79.

'I'm not a serpent!' said Alice indignantly. 'Let me alone!'<<Back

80.

'Serpent, I say again!' repeated the Pigeon, but in a more subdued tone, and added with a kind of sob, 'I've tried every way, and nothing seems to suit them!'<<Back

81.

'I haven't the least idea what you're talking about,' said Alice.<<Back

82.

'I've tried the roots of trees, and I've tried banks, and I've tried hedges,' the Pigeon went on, without attending to her; 'but those serpents! There's no pleasing them!'<<Back

83.

Alice was more and more puzzled, but she thought there was no use in saying anything more till the Pigeon had finished.<<Back

84.

'As if it wasn't trouble enough hatching the eggs,' said the Pigeon; 'but I must be on the look-out for serpents night and day! Why, I haven't had a wink of sleep these three weeks!'<<Back

85.

'I'm very sorry you've been annoyed,' said Alice, who was beginning to see its meaning.<<Back

86.

'And just as I'd taken the highest tree in the wood,' continued the Pigeon, raising its voice to a shriek, 'and just as I was thinking I should be free of them at last, they must needs come wriggling down from the sky! Ugh, Serpent!'<<Back

87.

'But I'm not a serpent, I tell you!' said Alice. 'I'm a--I'm a--'<<Back

88.

'Well! what are you?' said the Pigeon. 'I can see you're trying to invent something!'<<Back

89.

'I--I'm a little girl,' said Alice, rather doubtfully, as she remembered the number of changes she had gone through that day.<<Back

90.

'A likely story indeed!' said the Pigeon in a tone of the deepest contempt. 'I've seen a good many little girls in my time, but never one with such a neck as that! No, no! You're a serpent; and there's no use denying it. I suppose you'll be telling me next that you never tasted an egg!'<<Back

91.

'I have tasted eggs, certainly,' said Alice, who was a very truthful child; 'but little girls eat eggs quite as much as serpents do, you know.'<<Back

92.

'I don't believe it,' said the Pigeon; 'but if they do, why then they're a kind of serpent, that's all I can say.'<<Back

93.

This was such a new idea to Alice, that she was quite silent for a minute or two, which gave the Pigeon the opportunity of adding, 'You're looking for eggs, I know that well enough; and what does it matter to me whether you're a little girl or a serpent?'<<Back

94.

'It matters a good deal to me,' said Alice hastily; 'but I'm not looking for eggs, as it happens; and if I was, I shouldn't want yours: I don't like them raw.'<<Back

95.

'Well, be off, then!' said the Pigeon in a sulky tone, as it settled down again into its nest. Alice crouched down among the trees as well as she could, for her neck kept getting entangled among the branches, and every now and then she had to stop and untwist it. After a while she remembered that she still held the pieces of mushroom in her hands, and she set to work very carefully, nibbling first at one and then at the other, and growing sometimes taller and sometimes shorter, until she had succeeded in bringing herself down to her usual height.<<Back

96.

It was so long since she had been anything near the right size, that it felt quite strange at first; but she got used to it in a few minutes, and began talking to herself, as usual. 'Come, there's half my plan done now! How puzzling all these changes are! I'm never sure what I'm going to be, from one minute to another! However, I've got back to my right size: the next thing is, to get into that beautiful garden--how is that to be done, I wonder?' As she said this, she came suddenly upon an open place, with a little house in it about four feet high. 'Whoever lives there,' thought Alice, 'it'll never do to come upon them this size: why, I should frighten them out of their wits!' So she began nibbling at the righthand bit again, and did not venture to go near the house till she had brought herself down to nine inches high.<<Back
